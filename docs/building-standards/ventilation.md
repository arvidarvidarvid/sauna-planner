# Ventilation

> **Quick reference**
> | Parameter | Value |
> |-----------|-------|
> | Fresh air inlet size | **100–200 cm²** (≈ 10×15 cm opening) |
> | Inlet position | Low on heater wall, **below or just behind the stove** |
> | Exhaust vent size | **100–200 cm²** |
> | Exhaust position | Opposite wall from heater, **mid-height (0.5–1.0 m)** |
> | Air changes during use | **3–8 per hour** |
> | Post-sauna drying | Open door + vent fully, target 1–2 hours |

---

## Why sauna ventilation matters

Three separate purposes:

1. **Breathing air** — bathers need fresh oxygen. CO₂ builds up in a sealed room.
2. **Combustion air** — wood-burning stoves need oxygen to burn (electric heaters don't).
3. **Drying** — after use, the room must dry out completely to prevent rot and mold.

Poor ventilation is the #1 cause of a "stuffy" feeling in saunas (headaches, heavy air at
bench level). It's also the #1 cause of premature rot in sauna structures.

---

## Fresh air inlet

### Size
**100–200 cm²** free opening area. For a 2×2m sauna (~8.4 m³), 150 cm² is a good target.
This is roughly a **10 × 15 cm** opening or a standard round vent (Ø 140 mm ≈ 154 cm²).

### Position
- **On the heater wall**, low (0–300 mm above floor level)
- Ideally **directly below or behind the stove**
- The incoming fresh air is immediately heated by the stove and rises, mixing with the room air.
  This is the key mechanism — you don't feel a cold draft because the air is warmed before it
  reaches bather level.

### Why not on the opposite wall?
If the inlet is far from the heater, cold air streams across the floor and creates an
uncomfortable draft at foot level. Placing it near the heater eliminates this.

### Adjustable or fixed?
An adjustable sliding vent (spjäll) is ideal. During heat-up, it can be partially closed to
speed warming. During use, fully open. After use, fully open for drying.

## Exhaust vent

### The counter-intuitive placement

Most people expect the exhaust to be at the ceiling (hot air rises, right?). But in a sauna,
you **want** the hot air to stay up at the ceiling — that's where the bathers are.

**Correct placement: mid-height on the wall opposite the heater, 0.5–1.0 m above floor.**

This creates the ideal circulation pattern:

```
                    ┌───────────────────────┐
Hot air at ceiling  │ ←←←←←←←←←←←←←←←←←←← │  ceiling (hottest)
                    │                       │
Bather zone         │       benches         │  1.0–2.1 m (warm)
                    │                 [EXH] │  exhaust at 0.5–1.0 m
                    │                       │
Cool air at floor   │ →→→→→→→→→→→→→→→→→→→→ │  floor (coolest)
                    │ [IN]   [HEATER]       │
                    └───────────────────────┘
```

1. Fresh air enters low, near the heater
2. Heater warms it → it rises to the ceiling
3. Hot air flows across the ceiling to the far wall
4. It descends along the far wall
5. At mid-height, some exits through the exhaust
6. The rest continues down to the floor and back to the heater

This gives bathers a continuous supply of fresh warm air, with stale cooled air exiting
at mid-height — below the upper bench but above the floor.

### Size
**100–200 cm²**, matching the inlet. Use an adjustable vent.

### Alternative: low exhaust + separate drying vent

Some designs put the exhaust even lower (near floor, opposite wall from heater) and add a
separate high vent (near ceiling) that's only opened post-sauna for rapid drying. This can
work but the mid-height exhaust is simpler and serves both purposes.

## Air changes per hour

| Phase | Target ACH | Inlet | Exhaust |
|-------|-----------|-------|---------|
| Heat-up | 1–2 | Partially open | Closed or barely open |
| Active use | 3–8 | Fully open | Fully open |
| Cooling / drying | Maximum | Door open + vent open | Fully open |

For a 8.4 m³ room at 6 ACH: approximately 50 m³/h of air exchange. This is achieved naturally
(no fan needed) through the thermal stack effect — hot air inside creates positive pressure
that drives flow through the inlet and exhaust openings.

## Wood-burning kiuas: combustion air

A wood-burning stove consumes air for combustion (~10 m³/h for a small stove at full burn).
This creates negative pressure in the room, which can:
- Make the door hard to open
- Draw air backward through the exhaust vent (reversing the flow)
- Cause smoke spillage when opening the firebox

### Solutions

1. **Dedicated combustion air inlet** — a separate duct from outside directly to the stove's
   air intake. This is the best solution for airtight construction and may be required per BBR
   for airtight buildings. Some stoves have a connection point for external air.

2. **Shared inlet** — the general fresh air vent near the stove serves double duty. Works in
   reasonably airy construction but may not provide enough air during heavy firing.

3. **Door/window gap** — in loose construction with an older-style plank door, air infiltration
   is often sufficient. Not a reliable strategy for a well-insulated modern build.

**Recommendation for our sauna:** Use a **dedicated external air duct** to the stove base,
sized at **Ø 100–125 mm** (78–123 cm²). The general room ventilation inlet (10×15 cm) remains
separate. This prevents the stove from "stealing" the room's breathing air.

## Post-sauna drying

This is as important as the ventilation during use. A sauna that stays wet between sessions
will develop mold and rot within 2–5 years.

### Procedure after each use:
1. **Open the door wide** and the exhaust vent fully
2. If there's a separate high drying vent (near ceiling), open it
3. Leave duckboards propped up or removed so the floor can dry
4. Wipe down bench surfaces if practical
5. Leave everything open for **1–2 hours** (or overnight in summer)

### Design features that help drying:
- **Removable duckboards** — critical, already in our design
- **Interior counter-batten** behind panels — creates air gap so panel backs dry
- **No dead corners** — ensure air can circulate behind and under benches
- **Bench supports open** — use open leg/frame design, not solid skirting
- **No carpet, fabric, or moisture-trapping materials** anywhere

### Winter drying
In cold weather, the residual heat after a session drives drying effectively. Opening the
door for 30–60 minutes while the room is still warm is often enough. The dry cold air
absorbs moisture quickly.

---

## Ventilation and the glass door

Our sauna has a glass door from the entry room. Glass doors typically have less air
infiltration than traditional plank doors — this makes intentional ventilation more important.
Ensure the fresh air inlet and exhaust are properly sized and used.
